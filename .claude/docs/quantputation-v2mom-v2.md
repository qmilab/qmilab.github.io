## Company Introduction

### The Thesis

Three convictions drive everything we do at Quantputation:

**1. The complete world model requires quantum computers.**

Richard Feynman said it plainly in 1981: *"Nature isn't classical, dammit, and if you want to make a simulation of nature, you'd better make it quantum mechanical."* Nearly half a century later, this remains the most important unsolved problem in computing. A 300-qubit quantum computer can represent more states simultaneously than there are atoms in the observable universe. Classical simulations work well at macroscopic scales — weather, fluid dynamics, structural engineering — but they are approximations that break down at the quantum level. The complete world model, one that captures entanglement, superposition, and the full exponential state space of nature from molecules to materials to many-body systems, requires quantum hardware.

NLP and Transformer architectures have achieved extraordinary things — but they build world models in words. Even multimodal systems remain statistical learners over classical representations. They interpolate patterns in data. They do not simulate the physics that generates the data. Google's 13,000x speedup over the world's fastest supercomputer in simulating quantum interference effects isn't a benchmark curiosity — it's a demonstration that quantum hardware computes in the language of nature in a way classical hardware structurally cannot. The bridge from pattern recognition to genuine physical understanding runs through quantum computation.

**2. The rapid path to AGI runs through quantum computing.**

This is a contrarian position. We hold it with full conviction.

The classical AI scaling paradigm — more data, more parameters, more compute — is producing remarkable capabilities but diminishing returns on genuine understanding. Transformer-based models can generate, predict, and classify. They cannot simulate molecular dynamics, model many-body quantum systems, or reason about the physical world from first principles. If general intelligence requires understanding the physical world — not just producing plausible text about it — then it requires the computational substrate that matches reality.

The evidence is converging from multiple directions. On the theoretical side, Penrose and Hameroff's Orchestrated Objective Reduction theory — that consciousness arises from quantum processes in neuronal microtubules — gained experimental support in 2025. Hartmut Neven, who leads Google's Quantum AI Lab, is actively exploring whether consciousness has quantum origins. On the pragmatic side, AGI that reasons about chemistry, biology, materials science, and physics needs quantum simulation — classical approximations of quantum phenomena scale exponentially in cost and eventually become intractable. Both lines of evidence point the same direction: general intelligence and quantum computation are not separate problems.

**3. Quantum isn't just computing. It's an entire technological revolution.**

Quantputation's interest spans the full spectrum of quantum technologies:

- **Quantum Computing** — Algorithms, circuits, hybrid quantum-classical systems, quantum machine learning
- **Quantum Communications** — Quantum key distribution, quantum networking, information-theoretically secure communication channels (ESA's Eagle-1 QKD satellite launches in 2026; SpeQtral's entanglement-based CubeSat is already in orbit)
- **Quantum Sensing** — Gravitational mapping, deep-space navigation, precision timing (NASA and Infleqtion are flying the world's first quantum gravity sensor to space)
- **Quantum Memory** — Long-lived quantum states, quantum repeaters, the backbone of a future quantum internet
- **Quantum in Space** — Autonomous mission planning, AI-driven exploration, quantum-enhanced navigation

We believe future deep-space missions will not be run by humans on decades-old flight computers. They will be run by autonomous AI systems with quantum-enhanced computational power — systems that can reason, adapt, and optimize in environments where communication latency with Earth makes remote control impossible and where the physics of navigation, sensing, and resource optimization outstrips classical capability. NASA's QuAIL lab estimates quantum AI could reduce interplanetary mission planning time by up to 90%. ESA, NASA, and commercial ventures are already flying quantum hardware in orbit. The convergence of AI, quantum, and space isn't speculative — the engineering has started.

### The Gap

Quantum computing investment hit $3.77 billion in the first nine months of 2025 alone — nearly triple all of 2024. IBM's Qiskit community has 550,000+ users. Google, IBM, Microsoft, and Amazon — alongside state-backed programs in China, Europe, and India — are all racing toward quantum advantage.

And yet:

- The global quantum workforce is roughly 30,000 people against a projected need of 250,000 by 2030
- There is no centralized platform for discovering, sharing, and collaborating on quantum circuits and algorithms
- Moving a quantum use case from research to production takes 6-9 months of specialized work
- The tools are fragmented, the learning curve is brutal, and the community is scattered

This is where Quantputation starts — by building the infrastructure that closes these gaps.

### Where We Start: QubitHub

Our first product, **QubitHub**, is the developer infrastructure layer for quantum computing — the platform where quantum algorithms are discovered, shared, executed, and benchmarked. A place where a researcher can run a quantum circuit in their browser without installing anything, where an educator can share reproducible experiments, where an organization can benchmark algorithms across backends.

QubitHub is live at [qubithub.co](https://qubithub.co). It is the beachhead. What follows is larger.

### The Research Foundation: QMI Lab

Quantputation's scientific foundation is provided by **QMI Lab** (Quantum & Machine Intelligence Laboratory) — an independent research laboratory that investigates the intersection of quantum computing and machine intelligence. QMI Lab and Quantputation share a founder and a conviction, but serve different purposes.

QMI Lab publishes research openly across three pillars: Foundations of Machine Intelligence (classical ML/DL/NLP), Quantum Machine Intelligence (hybrid quantum-classical architectures, QML benchmarking), and Quantum World Models (quantum simulation meets learned representations). Its findings flow into Quantputation when commercially relevant — validating our thesis through peer-reviewed science, producing reference implementations for QubitHub, and advancing the algorithms that will run on tomorrow's quantum hardware.

Quantputation is the commercial vehicle. QMI Lab is the scientific engine. Together, they represent the full arc from foundational research to developer infrastructure.

## Mission Statement

**Make quantum technologies accessible, affordable, and portable — for everyone.**

These three words are a causal chain — each enables the next:

**Accessible** is where we start. QubitHub lets anyone run a quantum circuit in their browser without installing SDKs, configuring credentials, or understanding hardware specifics. Lower the barrier to entry first — everything else follows.

**Affordable** follows from accessibility. When quantum algorithms are hardware-agnostic and platforms are vendor-neutral, competition drives cost down. When more people can use quantum technologies, economies of scale emerge. Accessibility creates the conditions for affordability.

**Portable** is the hardest problem and the longest arc. We mean it across three horizons:

- **Software portability (now):** Write a quantum algorithm once, run it on any backend — superconducting, trapped-ion, photonic, or simulated. No vendor lock-in, no rewriting. This is what QubitHub delivers today.
- **Deployment portability (emerging):** Quantum hardware rugged and compact enough to operate outside the lab — in data centers, on satellites, aboard spacecraft. This is already underway: Infleqtion and NASA are flying cold-atom quantum sensors to orbit, SpeQtral's entanglement-based CubeSat is operational, and photonic systems run at room temperature. Quantputation intends to build the software layer that makes these deployable systems programmable and interoperable.
- **Physical miniaturization (open frontier):** Quantum capabilities in form factors approaching consumer devices. No credible path exists today — current hardware requires cryogenic cooling, vacuum chambers, or precision laser systems. We are honest about this horizon: it is a physics problem before it is an engineering problem, and the timeline is unknown.

## V2MOM

### Vision

**Create the global infrastructure that makes quantum technologies as accessible as classical computing is today — and serve as the commercial bridge that brings quantum-AI research from the lab to the developer.**

We envision a world where:
- Any developer can run a quantum algorithm as easily as they deploy a web app
- Quantum and AI are not separate fields but a single, unified discipline
- Space missions are powered by autonomous AI systems running on quantum-enhanced hardware
- The tools to work with quantum technologies are open, accessible, and affordable — not locked behind million-dollar lab equipment
- The best quantum-AI research, validated by QMI Lab and the broader scientific community, reaches developers through production-ready tools on QubitHub

### Values

*Ordered by priority. When two values conflict, the higher one wins.*

**1. Conviction over consensus**
We hold positions that the mainstream hasn't arrived at yet — that AGI needs quantum, that classical world models are fundamentally limited, that the future of space is quantum-AI. We back our convictions with engineering, not just arguments. We'd rather be early than late.

**2. Accessibility without compromise**
Making quantum technologies accessible does not mean dumbing them down. It means removing unnecessary barriers — fragmented tooling, vendor lock-in, steep onboarding curves — while preserving the full power of the underlying technology. Accessible. Affordable. Portable. No asterisks.

**3. Open by default, durable by design**
Our platforms are open. Our community is transparent. Our research partner, QMI Lab, publishes openly. The advantages we build — network effects, execution data, community trust — come from being the place people choose, not the place they're locked into.

**4. First-principles engineering**
We don't inherit assumptions. Quantum is not classical with weird bits. AI is not search with neural networks. Space is not aviation with more altitude. Each domain demands thinking from the physics up. We hire and build for this.

**5. Long-term vision, short-term execution**
Our vision spans decades. Our execution spans weeks. We ship. We measure. We iterate. The grand vision means nothing without relentless, disciplined delivery against near-term milestones.

### Methods

**M1. Establish QubitHub as the default developer platform for quantum computing**
- Launch public beta (Q2 2026), iterate on community feedback, achieve product-market fit
- Build the content flywheel: circuits, algorithms, tutorials, benchmarks — all discoverable, forkable, executable
- Integrate with major quantum frameworks (Qiskit, PennyLane, Cirq) and hardware backends
- Achieve 10,000 registered users and 1,000 monthly active developers within 18 months of public launch

**M2. Productize quantum-AI research through QubitHub**
- Integrate QMI Lab research outputs into QubitHub as production-ready tools — quantum ML workflows, reference circuit implementations, and benchmark datasets
- Build quantum machine learning templates and pipelines natively within QubitHub, informed by QMI Lab's benchmarking results
- Feature QMI Lab's published algorithms as curated, executable showcases on the platform — demonstrating that cutting-edge research runs on QubitHub out of the box
- Surface practical challenges from QubitHub's development (execution bottlenecks, developer pain points, benchmark gaps) as research questions for QMI Lab

**M3. Expand into the full quantum technology stack**
- Add quantum communications protocol simulation to the platform — starting with QKD protocol visualization and benchmarking, which reuses QubitHub's existing circuit execution infrastructure
- Build quantum sensing analysis tools, beginning with datasets from publicly available satellite and navigation experiments
- Create educational pathways that span computing, communications, sensing, and applications — content that compounds the platform's value and community

**M4. Develop the quantum-space capability**
- Build proof-of-concept quantum optimization tools for mission planning problems (traveling salesman variants, resource scheduling) using QAOA on QubitHub's existing execution layer
- Apply for quantum-for-space research grants (NASA SBIR, ESA BIC, DARPA) as funding mechanism and credibility signal
- Publish applied research connecting quantum algorithms to specific space operations challenges, in collaboration with QMI Lab where relevant
- Develop relationships with commercial space companies (trajectory optimization, autonomous navigation) as concrete entry points

**M5. Build a world-class, conviction-driven team**
- Hire across quantum physics, systems engineering, developer experience, and product
- Build a culture where contrarian thinking is expected, not tolerated
- Establish Quantputation as a destination for builders who want to ship quantum tools, complementing QMI Lab as a destination for researchers who want to advance quantum science
- Grow from founding team to 15-20 people within 24 months of seed funding

**M6. Establish and maintain the Quantputation–QMI Lab partnership**
- Formalize the upstream/downstream relationship: QMI Lab produces research, Quantputation commercializes relevant outputs through QubitHub
- Co-develop the research-to-product pipeline: identify which QMI Lab findings translate into platform features, reference implementations, or developer tools
- Leverage QMI Lab's publications as thesis validation when fundraising — peer-reviewed science de-risks Quantputation's commercial bet for investors
- Support QMI Lab's computational needs through QubitHub infrastructure where feasible

### Obstacles

**O1. Market timing risk**
Quantum hardware is still noisy, limited in qubit count, and expensive to access. Fault-tolerant quantum computing is likely 3-5 years away. We must build value in the NISQ era while positioning for the fault-tolerant era — without running out of runway.

**O2. The "too early" perception**
Many investors and potential hires believe quantum is 10+ years from maturity. Our thesis — that the infrastructure layer should be built now, before the hardware is ready — requires active evangelism. We must demonstrate near-term value (simulation, education, community) while making the long-term case.

**O3. Platform encroachment**
Established players could add hub features to their existing platforms. If Qiskit adds social and discovery features, or if Hugging Face expands into quantum, the window narrows. Our advantage is focus — quantum-native design, vendor neutrality, and community-first development — but we must build network effects and content density before incumbents move.

**O4. Talent scarcity**
The quantum workforce gap is the industry's #1 bottleneck: ~30,000 practitioners against 250,000 projected demand by 2030. Recruiting quantum engineers and developer experience specialists is hard. We must compete for talent against Google, IBM, Microsoft, and well-funded startups. QMI Lab as a research partner helps attract talent who want both commercial impact and scientific credibility.

**O5. Hardware fragmentation**
Different qubit technologies (superconducting, trapped-ion, photonic, neutral atom) have different gate sets, connectivity patterns, and noise profiles. Building a truly portable platform that works across all backends is a significant engineering challenge.

**O6. Solo founder risk**
Quantputation is currently a single-founder company. Scaling from vision to execution requires building the founding team quickly. This is the most immediate obstacle and the first one to solve.

**O7. Demonstrating the quantum-AI convergence commercially**
Our core conviction — that AGI requires quantum — is not yet mainstream. QMI Lab's publications build the scientific case. Quantputation must build the commercial case: that developers want quantum-AI tools, that enterprises will pay for them, and that the platform model works. The scientific proof and commercial proof are complementary — we need both.
